WORLD EFFECT BENCHMARK · A SPECIAL CLASS IN 18 PERIODS

Rebuilding Japan: The Classroom Edition

— We gave Japan a medical checkup using data, and the results were surprising —

A special class for students: using 37 countries × 1,193 indicators, we investigate how national growth relates to national happiness — starting from "what is correlation?" and ending with several pieces of grown-up common sense collapsing. No numbers to memorize. The only thing to take home is how to question numbers. All six figures are drawn from the actual data.

Welcome: About This Class

Hello, everyone. Today's class is a little unusual. The topic is "a medical checkup for the country of Japan."

You've all had a checkup before: height, weight, eyesight, a blood test. Numbers that tell you how your body is doing. Here's the thing — you can do the same for a country. Research organizations around the world collect enormous amounts of numbers about nations.

Today we'll use a dataset called the World Effect Benchmark. It covers 37 developed countries, including Japan, across 1,193 different indicators. Some are the classics — life expectancy, prices. Others are stranger: "Did people in this country smile yesterday?" "Did they feel they learned something interesting?" A national checkup is a lot nosier than a school physical. And countries can't refuse the exam.

This class has three goals.

First, we'll use these 37 countries and 1,193 indicators to investigate the relationship between a country growing (its economy) and its people being happy. You'll watch several pieces of grown-up "common sense" collapse in real time.

Second, you'll learn how not to be fooled by data. This is a skill you'll use for entrance exams, for jobs, for reading the news, for the rest of your life. Honestly, this might be the most important part of today.

Third, at the very end, we'll see what the data says about what Japan is missing. Spoiler: the conclusion is not depressing. It goes like this — "Japan is not sick. Japan has just gotten into the habit of not standing up from its chair." What that means will become clear as we go.

One ground rule before we start. Please don't memorize any numbers from this class. They won't be on a test. What I want you to take home is not the numbers, but the way to question numbers. If you keep that and forget everything else, this class was a success.

Let's begin first period.

Period 1: What Is a National Checkup?

First, let's look properly at our material.

Our subjects are 37 countries — the members of the OECD, which is basically a club of developed nations: Japan, the United States, Germany, France, Korea, Mexico, Iceland, and so on. There are nearly 200 countries in the world, but only about 37 keep statistics reliable enough for careful comparison.

For each country, we prepare two "report card grades."

Grade one is economic: the GDP growth rate. GDP is the total value of everything a country produces in a year — think of it as the country's earning power. We use the average yearly growth from 1999 to 2019, about twenty years.

Grade two is happiness. Between 2020 and 2023, people in each country were asked questions like "Did you enjoy yourself yesterday?" and "Did you smile or laugh yesterday?" — and we use the share of positive answers. You might object: "Can you even measure happiness with a number?" Great question. Not perfectly, no. But if you ask tens of thousands of people the same question and average by country, real differences between countries do show up. A thermometer isn't perfect either, but it can still tell you who has a fever.

Where do all these numbers come from, by the way? Three main sources. Government statistics — the money-and-counting records like GDP and health spending. International surveys — for example, PISA, a test taken by fifteen-year-olds all over the world, with a questionnaire attached; one very important number later in this class comes from there. And opinion polls — actual interviewers asking thousands of people in each country, "Did you smile yesterday?" Somewhere in the world, right now, someone's entire job is asking strangers whether they smiled yesterday. I find that oddly touching.

Every number has errors, of course. Translations differ; people fib. That's why today we'll never lean on a single number — we'll check whether many numbers point the same way. One arrow snaps easily. A bundle doesn't.

Now, Japan's checkup results. Ready?

Japan's life expectancy is 84.0 years — first place out of 37. World champion. Japan also has the world's most extreme number of CT scanners (a kind of medical imaging machine), close to the most pharmacists, and the longest hospital stays. In short: Japan is the world champion of not dying.

And the other grades? Economic growth: fourth from the bottom. Happiness: eleventh from the bottom.

So: lying in the world's best-equipped hospital, living longer than anyone, not growing much, not smiling much. And one more thing — Japan's government debt, compared to the size of its economy, is also the largest in the developed world. The world's longest lives and the world's biggest debt, in the same country.

Sounds gloomy? Don't worry. The conclusion of this class is not gloomy. But to get there, we first have to learn how to read data.

Period 2: What Is Correlation? — Ice Cream and Heatstroke

Here is the most important tool of the day: correlation.

Correlation means "the degree to which two numbers move together." Example: ice cream sales and heatstroke cases. On days when ice cream sells well, more people get heatstroke. On days when it doesn't, fewer do. The two move together — they are correlated.

We measure the strength with a "correlation coefficient," written as r, ranging from −1 to +1.

- r near +1: when one is high, the other is high (they move the same way) - r near −1: when one is high, the other is low (they move opposite ways) - r near 0: no relationship (they move independently)

Rough guide: above 0.2 means "looks like a relationship"; above 0.5 means "pretty clearly related."

Now, the part I want to say loudly:

Correlation is not the same thing as causation.

Back to ice cream. Ice cream sales and heatstroke are strongly correlated. Does eating ice cream cause heatstroke? Of course not. The real culprit is temperature. On hot days, ice cream sells and heatstroke rises. Both are effects of heat; neither causes the other.

The hidden puppet-master — the "temperature" in this story — is what statisticians call a confounder. Let's just call it the hidden boss.

A more school-flavored example: "Kids who eat breakfast get better grades." You've heard this one. It's a correlation. But it doesn't prove breakfast raises grades. Families where breakfast reliably appears may also be families with steadier routines and more homework help. The boss might not be breakfast. It might be the household.

Two practice problems. Headline: "Children who read more books earn more as adults." Find the boss. …How about parents' income? Families that can buy lots of books can also afford more education. Second problem: "Sports teams with morning practice win more tournaments." …Maybe strong teams are the ones motivated to hold morning practice. The arrow might point backwards! Suspecting a reversed arrow is also part of the skill.

So: whenever you see a strong correlation, reflexively ask three questions. Is there a hidden boss? Could the arrow point the other way? Could it just be a coincidence? Today we'll ask these three questions about an entire planet's worth of data. And once you can do this, about half the claims you see in news and advertising will start looking… suspicious. That feeling is called statistical literacy, and you're already acquiring it.

One last note: r has weaknesses. It only measures straight-line relationships, and one extreme country can drag it around. So in the analysis ahead, we double-check everything by converting values to rankings and re-measuring. Knowing your tool's weak points is part of using the tool.

Period 3: Data Wears Makeup — the Catch-Up Effect

National data has one boss bigger than all the others. Its name: the catch-up effect. Let's unmask it now, before it causes trouble.

Question: who will grow taller in the next year — a seventh grader or a twelfth grader?

The seventh grader, almost certainly. Someone who has already finished growing has less room to grow. Obvious, right?

National economies work exactly the same way. Countries that are already rich (the U.S., Germany, Japan) grow slowly. Countries still catching up can copy the leaders' technology and methods, so they grow fast. That's the catch-up effect: the ones behind can run faster.

Think of a marathon. The lead pack is already near its limit; their pace barely changes. Runners behind can study the leaders' pacing and drafting, and shave whole minutes. So if you ask "who has the fastest split right now?", you will not find the strongest runner — you'll find whoever is mid-catch-up. National growth rates behave exactly like this.

Why is this boss so troublesome? Because it applies makeup to nearly every correlation in the dataset.

For example, the data contains this: "Countries with longer life expectancy grow more slowly." Correlation: −0.59 — quite strong. Seeing this, you'd be tempted to conclude, "Longevity drags down the economy… aging societies are doomed…" Adults say this on TV constantly.

But wait. Which countries have long life expectancy? Rich ones, with good healthcare. And rich countries — like twelfth graders — have little room left to grow. So:

Long life ← because rich Slow growth ← because rich (little room left)

Both might just be effects of being rich. Ice cream and heatstroke again.

How do we check? Next period, we learn to remove the makeup.

Period 4: How to Remove Makeup — Computing "What If Wealth Were Equal?"

The idea for removing a boss's influence is simple: compare under equal conditions.

With height, the mistake was mixing seventh and twelfth graders. Compare seventh graders with seventh graders, and the growth-spurt difference disappears. For countries, we ask: "Among countries that are equally rich, does the relationship still hold?"

Statistics has a tool that does this mathematically, called partial correlation. You'll learn the formula in college; the meaning is simply "the correlation that remains after the boss's influence is subtracted." The face after the makeup comes off.

In this analysis, we picked five bosses and removed all their makeup at once.

One: wealth (GDP per person) — the biggest boss. Two: whether the country is post-communist. The Eastern European countries switched systems a few decades ago and are in the middle of a furious catch-up sprint, so they need special handling. (Yes-or-no traits like this get converted to 1s and 0s — called a dummy variable — so that even non-numeric things like history and region can join the math. Neat trick, right?) Three: whether the country is Latin American. People in Mexico and its neighbors, for whatever mix of cultural reasons, answer happiness surveys cheerfully almost no matter what. Four: whether the country is Nordic. Denmark and its neighbors score beautifully on any institutional measure, so they too get special handling. Five: the age structure — what share of the population is working-age.

Then we added three mean tests on top.

Mean test one: remove one country at a time and recompute — 37 times. If a correlation dies the moment Israel leaves the room, it was never a law of the world; it was a fact about Israel. We only trust relationships that survive every removal.

Mean test two: convert values to rankings and re-measure, to stop one extreme country from faking a pattern.

Mean test three: roll call of sibling indicators. If several different measures of the same idea (say, job training, measured separately for different skill levels) all point the same way, we trust it more. A single number shouting alone gets ignored.

And one final ally: the arrow of time. A cause must happen before its effect. Our happiness scores are from 2020–2023, while most of the 1,193 indicators were measured in the 2010s. So "the country became happy, and that caused the training programs of five years earlier" is a tough sell — effects don't travel backwards. When in doubt about an arrow, check the calendar.

Even after all this, we do NOT have proof of causation. I want to be honest about that. Proof needs experiments, and you can't split the world into two groups and change the laws in only one. But a relationship that survives makeup removal, 37 interrogations, ranking checks, sibling roll call, and the calendar is a strong causal candidate. Whenever I say "X boosts Y" today, silently append: "…is a strong candidate for boosting."

The toolbox is ready. Let's see the results.

Period 5: The Death of Common Sense — Correlations That Vanished

When the makeup came off, adult common sense started dying in the most entertaining way. In order of funeral:

First: "Longevity is the enemy of growth." Raw correlation: −0.59. After makeup removal: −0.12. Nearly gone. The boss was wealth, exactly as we suspected. Long life itself is basically innocent. Japan is the world's longest-lived country, but it can no longer use that as an excuse for slow growth.

Second, even more dramatic: the raw data showed "countries with more high blood pressure grow faster" at +0.65. A rather scary story suggests itself — work yourself sick and your country prospers!

After makeup removal: −0.01. Absolute zero. Countries in mid-catch-up simply happened to have high blood pressure — lives in upheaval, stress everywhere. Growth and hypertension were both effects of "catching up." Ice cream and heatstroke. The heroic tale of growth-through-self-destruction is, statistically, a phantom.

"Inflation accompanies growth" (+0.52) also nearly vanished (+0.06). "Tourist countries stagnate" mostly vanished too — and here the data threw us a gift: tourism strength, after makeup removal, turned out to be linked with happiness at +0.52. A country worth visiting is a country whose residents are in a good mood. That makes sense when you think about it.

Finally, a museum piece. The raw data contains this gem: "Countries with more new HIV infections have both higher growth AND higher happiness." …Obviously not a real effect. It was measured in only 21 countries and tangled in confounders — a pure coincidence.

Here's the big lesson. With 1,193 indicators, you're rolling 1,193 dice; some absurd correlations will appear by pure chance, guaranteed. You could test this in your own classroom: collect forty people's birthdays, shoe sizes, math scores, and a few hundred other things, compute every correlation, and you'll find something like "students born early in the year have worse eyesight" — plausible-sounding and utterly meaningless. Data analysis is not the job of getting excited about the coincidences that appear. It's the job of estimating in advance how many coincidences should appear. Whenever someone starts a sentence with "according to the data," check whether they did that estimate. If not… suspicious.

One picture to summarize this period. The "common sense" of international comparison is mostly a face with makeup on. Remove the makeup, and some faces vanish. Some stay. And — here's the preview for the rest of class — sometimes a face appears that nobody had ever seen, because two forces were canceling each other out and hiding it. Statisticians call it a suppression effect. We'll call it an iceberg: nothing visible above the water, something enormous below. Today's biggest discovery comes out of that water.

Period 6: The World as a Classroom — 37 Countries as Students

Before more makeup removal, let's meet the class. A country's personality shows less in its averages than in where it's the world's outlier.

Lithuania. Growth rate: first in class. Happiness: second from the bottom. The file shows high tuberculosis mortality, high elderly institutionalization, high elderly suicide — and rock-bottom scores on "valuing leisure," "felt respected," and "politeness." A straight-A student who never returns a greeting on the way to school. All three Baltic states wear this face. Countries that sprint don't have breath left for smiling.

Korea. Patents: basically first in the world. Shipbuilding, green-tech inventions: towering. Happiness: third from the bottom, with company training and female managers in the cellar. The country that invents and doesn't smile. Japanese students may try to read this file as someone else's problem. It won't work. This isn't a stranger; it's a relative in the mirror.

Israel. Venture capital investment: world's highest. R&D spending, as a share of the economy: 6 percent, first by a mile. Happiness: dead last. "Smiled yesterday": bottom of the class; "sadness": top. The most entrepreneurial country on Earth is the least smiling one. Any government about to adopt "innovation" as its national slogan should be made to read this student's file. Twice.

Across the aisle, the cheerful students. Mexico: happiness at the very top — with the world's highest diabetes mortality, towering homicide statistics, and bottom-tier scores on press freedom and democracy. Dangerous, unfree, over-sugared, happy. Statistics goes respectfully quiet in front of Mexico. Costa Rica, also near the top: the country that abolished its army and spent the money on schools — its public education spending is the world's most extreme. A country that traded rifles for textbooks sitting near the top of the happiness table proves nothing scientifically. Poetically, it proves quite a lot.

Iceland: happiness champion. Water per person, renewable energy, World Heritage sites per capita — all outliers. A nation that spends its evenings soaking in hot springs is the happiest on Earth. Japan's hot springs are second to none, by the way. The difference is probably the time spent soaking.

The slow-growth crowd deserves a word too. Italy and Greece: history-and-tourism superpowers, dead last in growth. But remember period 3 — they got rich early, so small growth is partly just small room-to-grow. And tourism, as we saw, turns out to be happiness's friend. Unhurried countries have unhurried strengths. Judging a student by one subject is bad teaching — for countries too.

America: decent growth, decent happiness — and a wild file. World leader in drug-overdose deaths and methamphetamine use, bottom of the class in job tenure, vacation days, and attachment to one's own town. The richest and most rootless. America generously runs the "what if a society moves TOO much?" experiment on humanity's behalf. Remember that, because Japan is about to be diagnosed with the opposite condition — and the healthy dose is somewhere in between.

And Japan. The outliers: hospital-stay length, CT scanners, pharmacists, government debt, share of Buddhists, and — through some whim of the database — the intensity of fan devotion to idols (measured via BTS fandom). The bottom-tier items: adult intimacy, and agreement with the sentence "When I'm in a difficult situation, I can usually find a way out" — 58.6 percent, versus Colombia's 90.1.

Quietly living longer than anyone; passionately cheering for idols; unable to believe one's own life can be worked out. Keep this strange three-piece set in mind. We'll come back for it near the end.

Period 7: The Four Windows — Sorting 1,193 Indicators

Time to organize. Every indicator gets sorted into four windows, by its relationship to growth and to happiness.

Window 1, "Both": positive for growth AND happiness. Window 2, "Growth-first": good for growth, bad for happiness. Window 3, "Happiness-first": good for happiness, bad for growth. Window 4, "Both lose": negative for both.

The counts (before makeup removal): Both: 25. Growth-first: 67. Happiness-first: 141. Both-lose: 22. And 937 indicators that fit none — barely related to either.

Three lessons jump out.

First: about 80 percent of the world's indicators (937 of them) have almost nothing to do with either national growth or national happiness. The news panics daily about one indicator or another; most of them live in the suburbs of relevance.

Second: the "Both" window houses only 25 indicators — and look who lives there. Natural beauty. Employee enthusiasm. Roads. Public education spending. Women in management. Optimism about next year. No AI, no finance, no buzzwords. The truly delicious stuff, for a country, is astonishingly plain.

Third, the residents' personalities. Growth-first houses diligence, income-obsession, male smoking — a chain-smoking salaryman of the postwar era. Happiness-first houses enjoying life, interesting work, relaxed family norms. And Both-lose? Government debt, anxiety, sadness, militarization, water shortage. Notice the theme: weight. The weight of debt, of mood, of weapons, of thirst. What ruins countries is not evil but tonnage — a lesson that works for personal life, too.

Also: Happiness-first (141) is about double Growth-first (67), and across all 1,192 indicators, "correlation with growth" and "correlation with happiness" are themselves correlated at −0.35. In the raw data, the whole world seems to say: what helps growth hurts happiness. A cosmic tug-of-war.

Except… when the makeup comes off, most of that tug-of-war unravels. Catching-up countries grow fast AND, having no slack, smile little — the catch-up boss was manufacturing the "you can't have both" storyline all along. Most laws of the universe, on inspection, turn out to be shortages of control variables.

So: after honest makeup removal, does anything boost both growth and happiness at once? Next period is the climax of this class.

Period 8: The Big Discovery — Job Mobility Boosts Both

We washed all 1,063 usable indicators (some lacked enough countries), and demanded: strongly positive for both growth and happiness, surviving all five makeup removals and all 37 mean tests without a single sign flip. Essentially one indicator passed.

Employment mobility. In plain language: how easy it is to change jobs.

There's an indicator for "share of workers at their current employer less than 10 years" — how much people actually move between companies. After makeup removal: partial correlation +0.53 with growth, +0.37 with happiness. Its mirror twin — long tenure, the degree of "lifetime employment" — scores −0.53 and −0.37. Perfect mirror images.

A quick calibration, so the number lands properly. In cross-country comparisons, after subtracting five bosses, a surviving 0.5 is a big deal. Human societies are complicated; no single factor ever explains most of a nation's fate. In social science, a post-makeup 0.5 is a heavyweight — think of a single school subject that decides nearly half your entrance-exam result.

And here's the delicious part: this was almost invisible in the raw data. Mobility's raw correlation with growth: +0.15. Nearly nothing. Why? Rich countries change jobs more, and rich countries grow slower, so the effect was underwater — our iceberg. Everyone had been looking at the zero above the waterline and calling the iceberg a pool float.

Why would job mobility power growth? Everyone has different talents, but nobody's first job is guaranteed to be the place where their talent shines. In a mobile society, people keep flowing toward where they're most useful, so the whole country's talent arrangement keeps improving. In an immobile society, mismatches get sealed for life. Picture a soccer team where a natural striker is stuck playing goalkeeper forever — versus a team where positions can change. Which team wins the league?

Why does mobility make people happier? Even more relatable. In a society where you can leave, a bad boss is a chapter of your life. In a society where you can't, a bad boss is the whole book. Indeed, tenure length correlates with happiness at −0.61 — one of the strongest mood-killers in this dataset.

Try this thought experiment. Imagine your school announced: "No more class reshuffles, no more seat changes. The seat you're assigned in April is yours until graduation." If you happen to click with your neighbor — paradise. If not… And notice something subtle: just knowing the seat change exists makes a bad seat bearable. "This isn't forever" protects the heart. The core reason mobility boosts happiness probably isn't the moving itself. It's the existence of the option.

Now, Japan. Japan's degree of lifetime employment is the highest tier in the world; its average years-at-one-company likewise sit near the global summit. Join a company at graduation, stay until retirement — that's been the honored pattern, with a proverb to match: "Three years on a stone" (sit on a cold stone for three years and it will warm up — so persevere). A fine proverb. The modern Japanese edition just omits the second verse: by the time the stone is warm, your legs are too numb to stand.

Let me be very clear about what the data is NOT saying. It is not punishing hard work. Diligence as a value kept a positive link to growth even after makeup removal. What gets punished is hard work bolted to one chair. The same effort, movable to where it's most alive, produces more — for the person and the country. Japan doesn't have surplus people. It has misplaced people — like the excellent pickles at the back of the refrigerator: everyone remembers they exist; the act of retrieving them never quite happens.

Nor is this an insult to the generation that built lifetime employment. In postwar Japan, it was a masterpiece: companies could safely invest in training because workers stayed; workers could take out mortgages because companies kept them. Under those conditions, it was the right answer. The problem is that the conditions changed and the system stayed. Even masterpieces go stale if you never re-examine the script. The fault lies not in the past decision, but in the present failure to inspect it.

Anticipating two questions:

"If everyone job-hops, won't companies stop training people? Why train someone who'll leave?" Excellent question — adults argue about this constantly. Data answers: the most mobile societies, Denmark and its Nordic neighbors, are ALSO world leaders in adult workplace training (coming in period 10). Immobile Japan and Korea are at the bottom of both. In the real world, it's not "loyal societies train more." It's "mobile societies train more." The logic: when everyone can leave, a company that doesn't develop people can't attract them. Training becomes a survival strategy. When nobody leaves, you can neglect people and keep them anyway. Intuition and statistics disagree here — and that disagreement is where learning lives.

"So people who spent their whole career at one company were wrong?" No. This is not about individuals. If your place fits you, staying is winning. The problem is people who don't fit and can't move. Staying by choice and staying by lack of choice may look identical on a résumé — thirty years at one firm — but they are opposite lives. The data isn't cheering for job-hopping. It's cheering for the ability to choose.

Denmark, by the way, made dismissal easy but wrapped it in generous unemployment support and free retraining — a system called flexicurity (flexibility + security). In other words: a floor you can stand up onto without dying. People whose legs have gone numb from kneeling don't need a lecture about standing. They need a softer floor. Denmark's happiness is perennially top-tier, and — remember this for later — the share of Danish fifteen-year-olds who believe "I can find a way out of difficulties" is 90 percent.

Period 9: What Does Overtime Buy?

This next finding may sting a little, especially for your parents. Deep breath.

Weekly working hours — how long people in each country work. Raw data: correlation with growth, +0.31. "See?" says a voice. "Countries that work hard, grow!" The voice is coming from an office at 9 p.m. on a Friday.

Makeup off: +0.08. Zero. Noise.

Long-working countries weren't growing because they worked long; catching-up countries happened to work long. Hold the development stage equal, and the length of the workweek buys not one gram of growth.

So do long hours buy nothing at all? No — they reliably buy one thing.

Unhappiness. Hours versus happiness: −0.57, and this one gets CLEARER after makeup removal, and shrugs off all 37 mean tests.

Summary: extending working hours is a purchase where you pay the fee (happiness) and receive no goods (growth). If a store did this to you, you'd call a consumer hotline.

Think about it through club activities. Two baseball teams: one drills "as long as possible," every night till eight; the other ends sharply at five-thirty but packs its practice with focus. Which gets stronger? Sports science has largely settled this: past a certain point, tired practice is bad practice — quality drops, injuries rise, thinking stops. "Hours practiced" and "strength" disconnect. Countries appear to work the same way.

Then why can't Japanese workplaces stop? One reason: measurement. "What did you produce?" is hard to measure. "How many hours were you here?" is easy. So effort gets graded the easy way. Imagine if your studying were graded not by problems solved but by hours at your desk. You'd get very skilled at… sitting at desks. Humans behave the way they're measured. Memorize that law — it will explain half of every organization you ever join.

There's even a built-in answer check for this hypothesis. Korea capped the workweek at 52 hours in 2018. If today's analysis is right, Korea's happiness should improve in the coming years without much damage to growth. In a few years, search for the World Happiness Report and check. Watching a statistical prediction succeed or fail in real time is the best part of this whole subject.

One more item. Military manpower (soldiers per population) correlates with happiness at −0.78 — the single strongest mood-killer in the entire dataset. Drop Israel from the data: unmoved. Drop Korea: unmoved. Conscription and overtime have something in common: time you didn't choose. Compulsory time, in any uniform, seems to corrode the human mood.

Period 10: The Timing of Learning — This One Is About You

Now, the most beautiful finding of the day. "Beautiful," in statistician-speak, means the results split perfectly in opposite directions. And this one concerns you directly.

First: children's study time — how long students study in each country. After makeup removal: growth +0.41, happiness −0.44. Countries that make kids study long hours do grow. And don't smile. This is one of the few genuine trade-offs that survived everything — a real "you can't have both."

Second: adult learning — the share of workers who receive training arranged by their employer. Result: happiness +0.83.

Let me explain what +0.83 means here. Excluding indicators that basically ARE happiness ("did you feel positive yesterday"), this is the strongest happiness link among all 1,063 indicators. Adults being taught new things at work. It survives every mean test; it holds for low-, mid-, and high-skilled workers alike; and its sibling — "I actually use reading and writing skills in my daily work and life" — stands right beside it at +0.82. To learn, and to use it. That pair is the best predictor of a nation's mood that this dataset contains.

And crucially: adult learning does NOT hurt growth (effect near zero). In the raw data it looked like a "happiness-first" resident that sacrificed growth — makeup again. Innocent. Teaching adults buys happiness at no growth cost. Effectively free.

So:

Learning flips its sign depending on WHEN it's given. Crammed into a fifteen-year-old: buys growth, paid for in happiness. Given to a forty-five-year-old: free happiness.

Now, Japan. Japanese fifteen-year-olds study among the longest hours in the world. The juku — cram school — is a double-schooling invention rare on this planet: school ends, and children enter a second school. At 10 p.m., station squares fill with sleepy uniforms. In passion for children's study time, Japan is world class, no contest.

And Japanese forty-five-year-olds? Corporate spending on employee development lags the other advanced economies badly — a gap that Japan's own government white papers have acknowledged for years. Savor that: acknowledged for years. Written annually, unchanged annually.

To be clear — this is NOT "you don't need to study." For you as an individual, academic ability opens doors; that hasn't changed. This is about a country's allocation. A nation that spends its learning-passion 100-on-children, 0-on-adults is buying less total happiness than one that spends 70–30. Your studying lost none of its value today. If anything, you gained an option: becoming an adult who keeps learning.

And here's the gift you can unwrap today. "Yesterday, I learned something interesting" correlates with happiness at +0.80 even in the raw data — and it has almost NO relationship with national wealth. Rich or poor, everyone qualifies for yesterday's small "oh, I get it now!" The cheapest ingredient of human happiness is a small moment of understanding. And no — it doesn't have to be schoolwork. A new trick in a game, a new move in club practice, finally understanding the lyrics of your favorite song. The feeling of "I know something today that I didn't know yesterday" — that's the ingredient. On nights when studying feels crushing, remember: learning itself is one of the most reliably happiness-linked activities humans have. What hurts isn't the learning. It's the way it's being stuffed in.

Period 11: Air, Hospitals, Courts — Happiness Handed Out for Free

When people discuss happiness, they usually reach for mindset advice: gratitude, contentment, positive thinking. But the data's list of what actually conditions a nation's mood is almost comically practical. Three items.

One: air. PM2.5 — fine-particle air pollution — versus happiness: −0.65, sharper after makeup removal, unbudged by any country's removal. Under dirty air, people don't smile. Flip it around: clean air is the quietest allowance a government can hand its citizens. No envelope, no thank-you — but it's deposited into everyone's account, daily.

A history note. Japan's air today is fairly clean, but in the 1960s and 70s Japan was world-famous for pollution — Yokkaichi asthma, photochemical smog; you've met them in social studies. From there, decades of law and technology polished the sky to its current state. "Clean air" wasn't found; it was built, by the generation before you. The most invisible infrastructure is usually somebody's forty years.

Two: medicine. Health spending per person looked unrelated to happiness in the raw data. Makeup off: +0.52 — hidden in wealth's shadow. Knowing you'll be seen by a doctor when sick quietly holds up a nation's mood. (Japan's world-record CT scanner fleet has been called excessive — machines outnumbering their workload. Though as problems go, "too many hospitals" is a fairly luxurious one. Problems have social classes too.)

Three: courts. Rule of law — promises kept, disputes settled by procedure rather than fists — scores happiness +0.49 and growth +0.34. An honor student of the "Both" window. Meritocracy (being rewarded according to your work) runs alongside at +0.47.

Now, some good news for this classroom. Air, medicine, law: Japan sits in the world's top tier on all three. The air keeps improving; the universal insurance card is the envy of nations; the courts function.

Japan already owns the foundation of happiness. Owns it — and ranks eleventh from the bottom.

Because nothing has been built on top. The immovable employment of period 8, the empty-box overtime of period 9, the undelivered adult learning of period 10 — all sitting politely, in perfect kneeling posture, on a world-class foundation. Imagine a house lot where the ground improvement and concrete foundation were finished to perfection thirty years ago… and the family has been living on the bare foundation slab, on a picnic sheet, ever since. When the rain gets in, don't blame the ground.

Japan's problem is not scarcity. It's arrangement. That single line is today's entire diagnosis.

Let's post the price list, recess-style, before the next period. Government is, in the end, shopping.

THE REAL-BUT-EXPENSIVE SHELF: children's study time (growth +0.41, costs happiness −0.44); tolerated inequality (growth +0.50, costs happiness −0.22). Real goods. Real prices. Buy deliberately.

THE DEFECTIVE-GOODS SHELF: overtime (price: happiness −0.57; contents: growth +0.08 — packing foam); mass military manpower (price: happiness −0.78); oversized government (price: growth −0.61; contents: happiness +0.02 — another nearly empty box; return policy discussed next period).

THE EFFECTIVELY-FREE SHELF: adult learning (happiness +0.83, no charge); clean air (avoids −0.65); healthcare that reaches everyone (+0.52); rule of law (+0.49, growth bonus included); higher education for the young (growth +0.55, no happiness charge); and job mobility (growth +0.53 and happiness +0.37, sold as a set).

A nation's mood is stocked on the effectively-free shelf. For thirty years, Japan has walked straight past it to queue — politely, punctually — at the defective-goods register. Store announcement: Dear customer, have you forgotten anything on your list?

Period 12: Grown-Up Politics, Viewed Through Numbers

This period, we eavesdrop on the arguments adults have every election, and check them against data. Fair warning: today's numbers are mildly rude to both teams.

One eternal argument: big government versus small government. Big government: collect more taxes, provide thicker services — pensions, welfare. Small government: tax less, let the private sector run.

The data says: government revenue as a share of the economy correlates with growth, after makeup removal, at −0.61. Pension generosity: −0.60. Total tax burden: −0.49. The bigger the state's draw, the slower the growth — robust to every test we have. The small-government team is now cheering.

They should wait for the second half. Does big government at least buy happiness with all that growth it spends? Government size versus happiness: +0.02. Zero. Generous pensions: −0.13, also nothing. As far as this data can see, big government pays the growth bill and receives no happiness merchandise — the same defective-goods contract as period 9's overtime, photocopied at national scale.

One honest caution, in bold: this indicator has a real reverse-arrow suspicion. Maybe slow growth forces tax ratios up, rather than big government slowing growth. We controlled for aging, but the arrow isn't fully pinned. Big-government fans may shelter behind this caveat for now. The data will see you at the next screening.

Now, inequality — measured by the Gini coefficient. How it works: if everyone in your class had exactly equal pocket money, Gini = 0. If one student held ALL the money, Gini = 1. We use the after-tax version: the inequality that remains after the government has redistributed.

Post-tax Gini versus growth: +0.50. Versus happiness: −0.22. Inequality is a genuine trade-off that survived every single test. Countries that tolerate inequality grow. And smile less.

Why might inequality fuel growth? Where gaps are wide, the prize for effort and risk-taking is bigger, so people dare more. Why might it erode happiness? Where gaps are wide, the fear of falling is bigger, and comparison grinds people down. An accelerator and a brake — you can see one in each number.

Notice what happened here. In period 5, the "hardworking vs. lazy" tug-of-war died on the autopsy table. The tug-of-war that actually survived is about distribution. The dataset's grandest structure — growth versus happiness — reduces, after honest deconfounding, to the old question: how sharp do we let differences get? And THAT question has no statistical answer. It's a values question — one that democracy has to sweat over. Which means, in a few years: you.

You can practice before you get the vote. Think of a class meeting where opinions split completely — the culture-festival booth, sports-day rules. Who won: the loudest voice, or the person who brought evidence? If the loud voice won, you witnessed a miniature of politics without numbers. Democracy practice starts in the classroom. Volunteer to be the person who puts numbers on the desk. It's an unglamorous job, and it changes what gets decided.

One parting number. "The degree to which a culture teaches children obedience" correlates with growth at −0.50 after makeup removal. Obedient citizens are convenient for whoever's in charge, and expensive for the economy. "Do exactly as you're told" is not just a boring philosophy. Statistically, it's a poverty program. Thinking for yourself, it turns out, has macroeconomic value.

Period 13: The Country That Can't Say "I'll Manage" — 58.6% Means You

Now for the number I most wanted to show you.

PISA is an international survey taken by fifteen-year-olds around the world. The 2018 edition included this statement, separate from the academic test:

"When I am in a difficult situation, I can usually find a way out of it."

The share of fifteen-year-olds agreeing: Colombia, 90.1 percent — the highest. Denmark, 90.0. Japan: 58.6 percent. Dead last of the 37.

Colombia is a country that Japan outclasses on every safety statistic in the book. Nine out of ten Colombian fifteen-year-olds believe they'll find a way out. In the safest, longest-lived country on Earth, four out of ten believe there is no way out. Nowhere else in this dataset do objective danger and subjective helplessness stand so perfectly on their heads.

"Maybe it's just that one weird question?" — good instinct! Let's use period 4's sibling roll call. PISA also asked: "I can usually manage one way or another" and "I can handle many things at a time." Japan: bottom tier on all of them. The siblings all point the same way. This isn't a quirk of translation. It's real.

This feeling — "I'll manage somehow" — is called self-efficacy, and in our data its relatives (like mathematical self-efficacy) link to happiness at +0.47. Believing you can find a way out lives next door to being happy.

So why do Japanese fifteen-year-olds, of all the world's teenagers, feel this least? Here is where today's whole class connects. My view: self-efficacy is not a personality trait. It's a transcript — a record of times you actually found a way out. Stack up escapes, and you believe in escaping. Never get the chance, and you don't. Now look at the society around you through periods 8 to 10. The adults live in a system where you can't leave a bad fit — so the experience "I left, and it worked out" is in chronic short supply, society-wide. The overtime of period 9 eats the evenings where new attempts would happen. The missing adult learning of period 10 rations out the experience of "I couldn't do this before, and now I can." Children learn from watching adults. Can fifteen-year-olds who've grown up watching adults who don't believe "it'll work out" be expected to believe it themselves? That 58.6 is not your fault. It's an honest review of the scenery you were shown.

Now let's collect the three-piece set from period 6: quietly living longest, passionately cheering for idols, unable to believe one's own life can be worked out.

What is fan devotion — oshi-katsu? It is full-hearted support of someone else's challenge. Time, money, and emotion invested in the story of someone on a stage finding a way through. Which means: Japan has NOT lost its capacity for belief and support. It has transferred the entire balance into other people's accounts, where it is managed diligently, at full yield. The fan proverb "support your idol while you can" is urgent precisely because, on the back of the receipt, it says: "I cannot support myself." The world's most fervent fan culture and the world's lowest self-efficacy on the same national chart — I don't think that's a coincidence. The cheering power exists, in world-class quantities. Only its direction is fixed: outward.

If that's the diagnosis, then rebuilding Japan is a surprisingly modest engineering project: bend one penlight inward. Not by willpower — the data even contains a warning about that. Agreement with the slogan-like statement "believing in myself gets me through hard times" correlates NEGATIVELY with happiness after makeup removal. Belief without a track record doesn't work; posters don't amend transcripts. What amends transcripts is experience — which is exactly what period 8's soft floor and period 10's adult learning are: machines for mass-producing the experience of finding a way out.

One more strange fact. Japan is the world's great nation of drills. Earthquake drills, fire drills, memorized evacuation routes — nobody prepares for disaster like Japan. The only drill never once conducted is the life drill: if your current place stops fitting you, where do you go, and how? Nobody has practiced. There's no route on the map to practice with. The disaster with the highest probability of striking you — a bad fit — is the one disaster this drill-loving country never drills for.

You can start your own "it-worked-out savings account" today, without waiting for any reform. Go somewhere new alone. Cook something you've never cooked. Volunteer for the festival role you've never tried. Small is fine — the point is to add one line at a time to your own transcript: "first time; managed anyway." Nationally, 58.6 is a heavy number to move. Inside you, it can start moving this afternoon.

Period 14: Five Proposals for Rebuilding Japan

Diagnosis done. Prescription time — five proposals, built strictly from the candidates that survived everything. Effects listed in partial correlations; honest notes attached.

Proposal 1: Build a floor people can stand up onto (labor mobility). Expected effect: growth +0.53, happiness +0.37 — the only both-ways lever found. Contents: make moving between jobs easier, AND make it safe: income support while between jobs, free retraining, skills and pensions that travel with the person (the Danish flexicurity model). Also retire the vocabulary that calls job-changers "quitters" and "traitors." Warning: kicking away the chair without softening the floor is NOT this proposal. That's just cruelty.

Proposal 2: Stop treating overtime as proof of effort. Expected effect: releases the −0.57 happiness penalty; growth lost: +0.08, i.e., none. Contents: measure output, not hours-in-building. Korea is running the 52-hour experiment at its own expense — gratefully copy its homework. For you: you can rehearse this in school today. Grade your day by "what can I do now that I couldn't this morning?" — not by hours at the desk. Measuring yourself by time is a rehearsal for an overtime-shaped adulthood. Drop the habit while it's cheap.

Proposal 3: Deliver learning to adults. Expected effect: happiness +0.83 — the strongest substantive happiness factor in the data. Growth cost: none. Contents: tax breaks for companies that train; personal learning accounts for individuals; training INSIDE working hours, as work (outside hours, it's just proposal 2's overtime in a graduation cap). Redirect even three-tenths of the nation's cram-school passion toward the forty-five-year-olds. For you: when you job-hunt someday, ask every company: "What will you teach me?" A company that winces at that question is, statistically, a company that won't make you happy. Job applicants' questions are a quiet way of voting for the society you want.

Proposal 4: Keep polishing the foundation (air, medicine, law). Expected effect: avoiding −0.65; +0.52; +0.49. Contents: Japan already leads here; maintain and fine-tune. Limit: one self-congratulation per day, or proposals 1–3 escape your field of view. For you: today's clean air and universal insurance are artificial objects, built by previous generations over decades. Invisible infrastructure starts quietly breaking the moment maintenance stops. Historically, the countries that lost their prosperity were rarely the ones that failed to invent — they were the ones that stopped maintaining what they had.

Proposal 5: Keep arguing about inequality — with better ammunition. Contents: inequality is a real trade-off (growth +0.50, happiness −0.22), and no dataset can settle where to draw the line. That's democracy's job — soon, yours. One request: whichever side you take, keep one document on the table — the audit showing big government has not been buying happiness (+0.02). Compete on the DIRECTION of spending (proposal 3, say), not its sheer tonnage.

The spirit of all five in one line: Japan must move from a country that accumulates to a country that circulates — people, money, learning, seats. What this country lacks is not resources but rotation. The world's longest life expectancy means the world's longest time on the game clock. A player holding the tournament's longest clock, refusing to move any pieces, waiting for time to run out — in shogi that's borderline stalling. Fortunately, countries play without time limits. The move can still be made.

Period 15: Open Floor — Argue With This Class

A class you can't argue with is a sermon. So here are the objections I expect, with my answers. Find yours.

"Only 37 countries? That proves nothing." — Correct, and confessed from the start. One footnote though: 37 isn't a small sample OF developed countries. It's nearly ALL of them. The sample isn't undersized; the planet is underdeveloped. Fifty years from now, when a hundred countries have comparable statistics, someone will need to re-run today's analysis. That someone is plausibly in this room.

"Happiness differs by culture. Comparing countries is meaningless." — Half right, which is why we mathematically removed the Latin cheerfulness and the Nordic contentment before reporting anything. Rejecting all comparison because cultures differ is like refusing a physical because "everyone's body is different." Bodies do differ. Blood pressure is still worth measuring.

"You said correlation isn't causation — then you wrote prescriptions. Contradiction!" — Sharp. Here's the resolution. "Correlation isn't causation" has a right and a wrong usage. Right: "…so let's climb toward causation, step by step" — which is what periods 4 through 13 were. Wrong: "…so nothing can be known," followed immediately by somebody's uncle's personal anecdote. Correlation isn't causation, but anecdotes aren't even correlation. We climbed as far as observational data allows and drew a line: beyond here lies experiments and replication. Prescriptions offered from below that line, with the line clearly drawn, aren't a contradiction. They're the scientific method's honest posture. (But yes — every prescription today carries an invisible watermark: "based on candidates.")

"Doesn't the analyst's own bias sneak into the analysis?" — The scariest and best question in the room. Answer: yes. Every analysis contains choices — which bosses to control, where to draw thresholds. I've written all my choices into this class, but disclosure isn't neutrality. Which is why the strongest thing I can hand you isn't my conclusion — it's a reproducible procedure. Same data, same steps, same result, whether or not you trust me. Science is trustworthy not because scientists are noble but because the system welcomes re-checking. Don't believe. Re-compute. If you remember one sentence from today, that's the one.

"Does any of this actually concern us? Kids don't set policy." — Three ways it does. One: you'll vote in a few years; these numbers are your ballot-reading glasses. Two: you'll job-hunt, and your questions to companies — "will you teach me?", "do you measure hours or output?", "do you badmouth people who left?" — are quiet votes that companies count. Three: the 58.6 percent in period 13 is your generation's number, and unlike national statistics, your personal copy of it accepts deposits starting today.

Period 16: Doubt This Class — the Honest Fine Print

The last period is for doubting today's class itself. Never trust a teacher who skips this part.

Fine print one: everything today is correlation, not proven causation. There is no method in this universe for extracting proof from 37 observational data points. What survived our gauntlet are strong candidates. Candidates are candidates — prime suspects, not convictions.

Fine print two: 37 countries is small, and 1,193 indicators guarantee some coincidences. Remember the HIV exhibit from period 5 — we framed it and hung it on the wall precisely so you'd remember that this dataset manufactures the occasional absurdity. Statistically speaking, a few of today's findings will eventually be exposed as coincidences too. That's fine. Science isn't a collection of certainties; it's an error-finding machine that keeps running. Newton got partially rewritten by Einstein, and bridges built on Newton still stand. Claims that say "absolutely true" and claims that say "here's exactly how far we know" — in the long run, trust the second kind.

Fine print three: the answer-check calendar. Korea's working-hours cap: watch its happiness over the next few years. Denmark's flexicurity: watch the replications. And within Japan: prefectures differ in job mobility — checking whether the mobile ones are happier and faster-growing is a science-fair-sized project that would genuinely test today's biggest claim. I'm leaving these hypotheses with you, their right to fail fully guaranteed.

Also, some things today's class couldn't cover: climate; other ways of measuring happiness (by "life satisfaction," Japan ranks a bit higher than by daily emotion — measurement choices move rankings). One dataset is one snapshot. Solid objects require many photographs. Today was photograph number one.

Finally, my actual request. What separates you from someone who missed this class isn't knowledge about Japan. It's a reflex: the ability to pause for one breath in front of a strong number. Once more, the five lines:

Correlation is not causation. When you see a strong correlation, hunt for the hidden boss. Ask: "If conditions were equal, would this survive?" Never trust a claim built on one country, one case, one anecdote. And never trust a conclusion that arrives without fine print.

These five lines work on entrance-exam essays, on suspicious posts in your feed, on your future boss's slideshow, and on election season. Today's class pretended to be about Japan. It was actually about these five lines.

After Class: One Thing, Today

Thank you for sitting through a long class. Let me leave you with the dataset's most hopeful fact, one more time.

"Yesterday, I learned something interesting" is linked to happiness at +0.80 — and has almost nothing to do with how rich a country is. The strongest ingredient of happiness is handed out daily, free of charge, entirely outside the jurisdiction of GDP.

Rebuilding a country sounds like a job for parliaments and executives. But the +0.80 ingredient can be picked up this afternoon, by you, on the way home. Learn one thing you didn't know yesterday. Become able to do one thing you couldn't. It doesn't have to be schoolwork.

And you've picked up something else today, quietly: the tools. Correlation versus causation. Boss-hunting. Makeup removal. The one-breath pause. The question, "Would that survive with the makeup off?" The diagnosis of Japan will age and expire. The tools won't.

One honest prediction. When you're grown, you will still hear both voices — "Japan is finished" and "Japan is amazing" — each waving one or two heavily made-up numbers. When you do, remember this classroom. Not cheerleading, not despair: diagnose, then rearrange. A country isn't something you root for or give up on. It's something you maintain — and as inheritances go, the world's longest-running garden is not a bad one to be handed the shears for.

Japan built the world's longest life expectancy out of forty years of small, patient improvements. Point that same patience away from the length of life and toward the point of living, and let's see what happens. The people who will find out are the youngest readers of this data.

The last line of the chart, and class is dismissed:

— Findings: walking ability, no problems detected. The patient is simply invited to walk.
