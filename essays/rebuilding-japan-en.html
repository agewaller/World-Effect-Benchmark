WORLD EFFECT BENCHMARK · COMPREHENSIVE SCREENING REPORT

Rebuilding Japan

— Your results from the 37-country, 1,193-factor annual checkup —

We took the international comparison data of 37 OECD countries across 1,193 indicators, wiped off five layers of confounding makeup (development level, post-communist history, Latin America, the Nordics, age structure), and ran every survivor through 37 leave-one-out interrogations. What follows: the myths that died, the levers that surfaced, and the patient in the world's finest hospital bed. All six figures are drawn from the actual data.

Rebuilding Japan — Your Results from the 37-Country, 1,193-Factor Annual Checkup —

Prologue: Your Test Results Are In

Dear Japan. Thank you for your patience. Your test results have arrived, and I am here to walk you through them.

The examination you underwent was a comprehensive screening of the thirty-seven OECD member countries across one thousand one hundred and ninety-three indicators. From blood pressure to PhD attainment, from the share of Buddhists to "did you smile yesterday" — everything about a country that can be measured, we measured. A human physical gets by with height, weight, and bloodwork; a national physical asks whether the citizenry felt they learned something yesterday. Modern statistics is a remarkably rude invention. Worse, countries cannot decline the exam. International organizations are essentially a mobile health-screening van that shows up without an appointment.

Let me begin with the overall assessment.

Dear Japan: out of thirty-seven countries, you rank first in life expectancy. Eighty-four point zero years. World champion — congratulations. You can stay in a hospital bed longer than anyone on Earth, your CT scanner count is the global outlier, and your number of pharmacists is very nearly first as well. You are, quite simply, the country that has most successfully mastered the art of not dying.

And, the patient leans forward — what about growth? What about happiness?

Growth: fourth from the bottom of thirty-seven. Happiness: eleventh from the bottom.

In other words: you lie in the world's best-equipped hospital, living longer than anyone, growing very little, and not smiling much. In the margin of the chart, your attending physician has no choice but to write: "Life support: flawless. Something to live for: needs work."

Incidentally, your debt — gross government debt relative to GDP — is also the world's most extreme. No other country has managed to combine the world's longest life with the world's largest debt. This is not sarcasm; it is statistics. Or rather, statistics is sarcasm that arrived first.

This essay is the record of a thorough re-examination of that checkup data. We did not merely gaze at it. International comparison data, left unattended, tells lies. More precisely: it does not lie, but it lets you deceive yourself. Countries that were poor grow quickly no matter what (economists call this the catch-up effect; the countries doing the catching up call it "just working, normally"); Latin Americans report cheerfulness even where homicide statistics are dreadful; Nordics score beautifully on whatever institution you measure. These background effects apply makeup to every correlation in the dataset.

So we proceeded as follows. First, for all 1,063 usable factors, we mathematically wiped off five layers of makeup: level of development (GDP per capita), post-communist history, Latin America, the Nordics, and the age structure of the population. Next, we subjected every surviving correlation to a torture protocol: recompute thirty-seven times, dropping one country each time (statisticians politely call this a "robustness check"; in practice it is an interrogation titled "Can you still say that without Israel in the room?"). We then verified that each relationship held when values were replaced by ranks, that sibling indicators of the same concept pointed the same way, and that the alleged causes were measured earlier in time than the outcomes. None of this is a magic spell that proves causation. But on the scaffolding of observational data, these are all the rungs the ladder has, and we climbed every one.

When the makeup came off, two things happened. First, a number of causal stories we have believed for decades died — enjoyably. Second, from under the makeup emerged several faces nobody had been looking at.

If you want this essay's conclusion in a single line, here it is.

— Japan is not sick. Japan has merely acquired the habit of not standing up from its chair.

We begin with the obituaries.

Chapter 1: The Museum of Phantoms — An Exhibition of Dead Myths

Welcome to the museum. On display are correlations long beloved in the world of international comparison, each formally pronounced dead upon removal of makeup. Please follow the arrows.

Exhibit One. "Longevity is the enemy of growth." Deceased.

In the raw data, life expectancy correlates with GDP growth at minus 0.59 — a handsome inverse relationship. Commentators sighed about "the fate of mature societies"; investors muttered about shorting the elderly. In our own list of one hundred findings, this was crowned King of Paradoxes.

Remove the development-level makeup, and the correlation collapses to minus 0.12. The mechanism is embarrassingly simple: long-lived countries are already rich, and rich countries have little room left to grow. That is all. Longevity itself is essentially innocent of retarding growth. Dear Japan: your world-leading life expectancy can no longer be used as an excuse for your low growth. Condolences — or congratulations, whichever applies.

Exhibit Two. "Growth requires grinding yourself down." Deceased.

Raw data: hypertension rates correlate with growth at plus 0.65; deaths from noncommunicable diseases between ages thirty and seventy, plus 0.58. Raise your blood pressure, shave down your lifespan, and your country will grow — a narrative you can hear from any middle manager three drinks into an izakaya evening, and there it was, printed plainly in the data.

After the makeup came off, plus 0.65 became minus 0.01. Zero. Not a trace. It was never that hypertensive countries grow; it was that countries in mid-catch-up happened to have high blood pressure too. The aesthetic of self-sacrificial growth is, statistically, a phantom. Ladies and gentlemen: the next time your physician mentions your blood pressure, please do not reply that it is the price of economic development. The data has stopped covering for you.

Exhibit Three. "Inflation walks with growth."

Consumer price inflation and growth: raw correlation plus 0.52, a number that could move a central banker to tears. After deconfounding: plus 0.06. Catch-up makeup again — in a catching-up country, prices, wages, and everything else are in motion at once. Before betting the fate of civilization on a two-percent price target, a brief stop at this display case might have been worthwhile.

Exhibit Four. "Urbanized countries stagnate" and "Tourism countries stagnate."

Urban population share appeared to correlate with growth at minus 0.47; after deconfounding, minus 0.01. Tourism competitiveness at minus 0.65 shrank to minus 0.31. But here something curious happened: tourism competitiveness, in its relationship with happiness, surfaced after controls at plus 0.52. Countries good at tourism are less the enemies of growth than the friends of national mood. Which, on reflection, is obvious: a country worth showing to visitors is a country whose residents are in a decent mood. Ministries of tourism may use the following slogan free of charge: "Hospitality is exportable good humor."

Exhibit Five — the museum's centerpiece. "New HIV infections boost both growth and happiness."

Raw data: growth plus 0.67, happiness plus 0.52. A miracle? No. A textbook spurious correlation, measured in only twenty-one countries and marinated in confounders. We refused to delete it; we framed it instead. Throw a thousand indicators at thirty-seven countries and miracles of this grade will reliably bubble up. Data analysis is not the job of excitedly reporting the miracles that bubble up; it is the job of estimating, in advance, the bubbling rate. Admission to this museum is free, but that lesson is priced separately — please take it home with you.

This concludes the tour. The museum shop sells a teacup inscribed "Correlation is not causation."

Chapter 2: A Grand Tour of the Waiting Room — Thirty-Seven Patients

Before removing anyone's makeup, let us observe the other patients in the waiting room. A country's personality lives not in its averages but in the indicators where it is the world's outlier.

First, Lithuania. Growth rate 0.052 — head of the class, first of thirty-seven. Happiness: second from the bottom. The chart's standout items: tuberculosis mortality, elderly institutionalization, elderly suicide, and transmission-grid losses. Meanwhile "valuing leisure," "felt respected yesterday," and "politeness" all sit in the bottom tier. A straight-A student who greets no one on the walk to school. All three Baltic states wear this same face. Countries that run fast do not have breath left over for smiling — that is one edge of the raw-data world.

Next, Korea. Patents: essentially first in the world. Shipbuilding and green-tech invention: towering. Happiness: third from the bottom, with employer-provided training, female managers, and "not afraid to fail, would start a business" all in the cellar. A country that invents, and does not smile. Japanese readers may attempt to peruse this chart with the detachment reserved for other people's problems. They will fail. This is not "our neighbor ten years ahead"; it is a relative in the mirror.

Israel. Venture capital investment per capita: first in the world. R&D at 6.0 percent of GDP: first by a mile. Happiness: dead last of thirty-seven. "Smiled yesterday": bottom tier. "Sadness": top tier. Military manpower per capita: heavyweight class. The most entrepreneurial country on Earth, and the least smiling one. Before any government adopts "innovation" as a national slogan, its cabinet should be made to read this patient's chart. Twice.

Across the aisle sit the cheerful patients. Mexico: happiness at the very top. Open the chart: diabetes mortality first in the world, slum population and homicide rates towering, press freedom and liberal-democracy indices in the basement. Dangerous, unfree, over-sugared — and happy. Before this patient, statistics falls respectfully silent. Costa Rica, also near the top in happiness: the country that abolished its army, whose public education spending — where the military budget went — is the world's most extreme. A nation that traded rifles for textbooks sitting high on the happiness table proves nothing about causation, but it proves something about poetry.

Iceland. Happiness: first. Water resources per person, renewable energy, World Heritage density: all outliers. A nation soaking in geothermal baths is the happiest on Earth. Dear Japan — your hot springs concede nothing to theirs. The difference, one suspects, is the time spent soaking.

Switzerland is the world's vault — foreign reserves and gold holdings towering — and quite happy. The United States is the outlier in illegal drug deaths and methamphetamine use, while sitting in the bottom tier for job tenure, vacation days, and feeling attached to one's own town: the richest and most rootless of nations. America has kindly agreed to absorb, on humanity's behalf, all the side effects of moving too much, thereby furnishing the perfect control group for a country that moves too little.

Finally, Japan. Standout items: average length of hospital stay, CT scanners, pharmacists, government debt, share of Buddhists, and — through some whim of the database — the fervor of BTS fan activity. Bottom-tier items: frequency of sexual activity, and the statement "when I'm in a difficult situation, I can usually find a way out" — self-efficacy, at 58.6 percent agreement, against Colombia's 90.1.

Quietly living longest, passionately cheering for idols, and unable to believe one's own life can be worked out. Remember this peculiar three-piece set. We will visit it again just before the epilogue.

Interlude: The Four Windows — Where the World's Factors Live

Before the main argument, a look at the map legend. The 1,193 factors sort into four windows, by their effect on growth and on happiness.

Window one: SUCCESS — factors that lift both. Population: a mere twenty-five. The prime real estate of this world is astonishingly vacant. Its raw-data residents: natural capital, employee engagement, total road length, public education spending, the share of women in management, optimism about next year. Note the absence of a single flashy tenant. No innovation, no finance, no AI. The both-ways window is occupied by roads, enthusiasm, and hope. That the housing register of a nation's best address is this humble deserves far more publicity than it gets.

Window two: SPARTA — growth at happiness's expense. Sixty-seven factors: diligence, income ambition, male smoking rates, the suicide of the old rather than the young. In short: the postwar work ethic lives here, chain-smoking.

Window three: SPOIL — happiness at growth's expense. One hundred forty-one factors, the largest population of the four: hedonism, births outside marriage, finding one's work interesting, employer-provided training (later exonerated — see Chapter 5). Happiness appeared to quarrel with growth twice as often as the reverse.

Window four: SABOTAGE — both lose. Twenty-two factors: government debt, anxiety, sadness, militarization, water stress. The residents share one trait: weight. The weight of debt, of mood, of weapons, of thirst. What ruins nations is not malice but tonnage — that is this window's lesson.

The remaining 937 factors belong to none of the windows: SLIGHT, the vast suburbs of "measured, but moves neither needle." Seventy-nine percent of the world's indicators are suburbanites. Considering that the news cycle panics daily over one indicator or another, this population distribution rewards contemplation.

So much for the raw-data map. Now remove the makeup, and a remarkable share of the residents begin moving house. Employer training, wrongly jailed in SPOIL, is exonerated toward SUCCESS; work hours, the pride of SPARTA, are exposed as "buying no growth, only unhappiness" and deported toward SABOTAGE; and job mobility and young graduates, hiding in the suburbs, move into SUCCESS's prime addresses. The rest of this essay is a record of that relocation.

Chapter 3: The Culprit Was Never Hard Work — Lifetime Employment, or the Art of Sitting Seiza

Now, the main event: growth and happiness.

Running through all 1,193 factors is a structural tug-of-war — what helps growth tends not to help happiness (correlation: minus 0.35). As the waiting room showed, the world seems torn between the diligent, unhappy Baltics and the cheerful, slow-growing Latins.

Remove the makeup, and most of this tug-of-war is the catch-up effect in disguise. A country in mid-catch-up grows fast and, having no slack, smiles little. That mundane fact had been masquerading as a law of the universe: "growth and happiness cannot coexist." Most laws of the universe, on inspection, are shortages of control variables.

The question, then: after the makeup comes off, does any factor push both growth and happiness upward at once?

One does. Out of 1,063 factors washed and interrogated, essentially one.

Labor mobility. In plain words: changing jobs.

The share of workers with under ten years' tenure — how much a country's people actually move between employers — shows, after deconfounding, a partial correlation of plus 0.53 with growth and plus 0.37 with happiness. Its mirror image, long tenure (the degree of lifetime employment), scores minus 0.53 and minus 0.37. Across thirty-seven leave-one-out interrogations, the sign never flipped once; replace values with ranks and the relationship holds; every sibling indicator points the same way. This is the single both-ways lever the entire checkup produced.

And — deliciously — it was nearly invisible in the raw data. Mobility's raw correlation with growth: plus 0.15. Rich countries change jobs more, and rich countries grow slower, so the effect hid underwater. An iceberg, like education. We had been looking at the zero above the waterline and calling the iceberg a pool float.

Return now to Japan's chart. Your long-tenure rate is the highest tier in the world; your average years of service likewise sit at the global summit. Lifetime employment. Seniority pay. "Three years on a stone," says the proverb — sit on a cold stone for three years and it will warm. The modern Japanese edition omits the second verse: by the time the stone is warm, you can no longer stand up from it.

Here is the situation, plainly. The world's data says: stand up, change chairs, and the country grows while its people cheer up. Japan remains in seiza — the formal kneeling posture in which the legs go numb. Everyone knows the legs are numb; the sitter knows it best of all. And still no one stands, because everyone else is also sitting. In Japan, seiza is not a personal choice. It is the national seating arrangement.

Let there be no misunderstanding: the data does not punish diligence. Diligence as a value survives deconfounding with its growth link partly intact. What the data punishes is diligence bolted to a single chair. The same effort, in a society where it can migrate to where it is most alive, produces more per country than in a society where it is exercised until retirement in the first seat assigned. Economists call this reallocative efficiency. I prefer the plainer version: Japan does not have surplus people. It has misplaced people — like the excellent pickles at the back of the refrigerator, whose existence everyone remembers and whose retrieval never quite occurs.

The happiness mechanism is more human still. In a society where you can leave, a bad boss is a chapter. In a society where you cannot, a bad boss is the book. That difference cannot fail to show up in a nation's mood — and it does: length of tenure correlates with happiness at minus 0.61, high on this checkup's list of national mood-killers. Where the world's workers say "I quit," Japan's workers say "I want to quit." The verb sits one conjugation short of freedom, permanently.

Denmark decided to make dismissal easy and cushioned it with generous unemployment support and retraining, and named the arrangement flexicurity. Translated into Japanese circumstances: a floor you can stand up onto without dying. What a nation of seiza-sitters needs is not a lecture on standing. It is a softer floor.

Lifetime employment was once Japan's treasure, and this essay does not mock the era in which it was. But treasures, when the era turns, take up new employment as cursed artifacts. The irony writes itself: in Japan, the only thing that never changes jobs is employment itself.

Chapter 4: What Does Overtime Buy? — Answer: Nothing

The next finding may sting. Japanese readers are advised to take a breath.

Weekly working hours, all employed persons, by country. Raw data: correlation with growth, plus 0.31. See! — someone is saying — the countries that work, grow. The voice is coming from an office at nine on a Friday night.

Remove the makeup. Working hours versus growth, partial correlation: plus 0.08.

Zero. Noise. It was never that long-working countries grow; countries in mid-catch-up happened to work long. Hold development constant, and the length of the workweek buys not one gram of growth.

Then what do long hours buy? Something, certainly. The purchase is confirmed.

Unhappiness. Working hours versus happiness: minus 0.57. This one strengthens when the makeup comes off, and survives every one-country-removed interrogation without a flinch.

Let us total the receipt. As far as international data can show, the marginal extension of working hours is a transaction in which one (a) buys no growth and (b) reliably pays in happiness. In commercial terms: a contract where you pay the invoice and no goods arrive. One would normally refer this to a consumer protection agency, but overtime is not a consumer contract, so there is no one to call. The labor standards office, perhaps — but they are working overtime.

Note what is not being said. This is not the tired sermon that "the Japanese work too much." The data is more precise, and more interesting. Work itself is not the culprit; diligence as a value kept some of its growth link, as reported. The culprit is hours. Diligence-as-a-value and hours-as-measured-occupancy-of-a-chair turn out to be entirely different people who happen to share an office. Extending your time in the building proves loyalty to growth; it does not provide growth. On this distinction the data is ice-cold.

Consider: in how many countries does "busy" function as a greeting? In Japan, a request that fails to open with "I apologize for intruding on your busy schedule" verges on rudeness — the etiquette requires diplomatically deleting, in writing, the possibility that the recipient has free time. Busyness as currency, as honor, as alibi. Unfortunately, on the post-deconfounding exchange, this currency trades at zero growth and minus 0.57 happiness. We have been paying each other respect in banknotes the central bank of reality does not honor.

Conveniently, a falsification test is already running. In 2018, Korea introduced a fifty-two-hour weekly cap. If the "hours buy only unhappiness" hypothesis is right, Korean happiness should improve in the coming years without notable damage to growth. Consult the World Happiness Report a few years hence, as one checks an answer key. Rarely can one watch a statistician's prediction succeed or fail from such a comfortable seat — and rarely does a neighboring country volunteer to run one's experiment at its own expense.

One appendix. Military manpower correlates with happiness at minus 0.78 — the single strongest mood-killer in the entire checkup. Drop Israel: unmoved. Drop Korea: unmoved. Conscription and overtime: compulsory time, in whatever uniform, corrodes the national mood. Is that too neat a summary? Perhaps. But the data lines up in a way that begs to be summarized too neatly. At minimum, the absence of conscription may be one reason Japan's happiness clings to eleventh-from-bottom instead of lower. Even a bedridden patient is entitled to a small boast.

Chapter 5: A Country That Crams Its Fifteen-Year-Olds and Never Teaches Its Forty-Five-Year-Olds

Now for the most beautiful finding of the checkup. "Beautiful," in statistician dialect, means "the signs split perfectly."

First, children's study time — how long a country's students study. After makeup removal: partial correlation with growth plus 0.41; with happiness, minus 0.44. Countries that make children study long hours grow. And do not smile. This is one of the few genuine trade-offs to survive deconfounding, filed under SPARTA.

Next, adult learning — the share of workers receiving employer-provided training. Partial correlation with happiness: plus 0.83.

Zero point eight three. Excluding tautologies ("did you feel positive yesterday" predicting positivity), this is the strongest substantive correlate of national mood among all 1,063 factors. Adults being taught things at work. It survives every leave-one-out; it holds for low-, mid-, and high-skilled workers alike; every sibling indicator salutes in the same direction. And, for good measure: the felt use of reading and writing skills at work and at home also runs at plus 0.82. To learn, and to use what one learns — that pair is the best available predictor of a nation's mood.

Crucially, adult learning does not harm growth (partial correlation minus 0.15; effectively zero). In the raw data, employer training wore SPOIL's prison uniform — happiness at growth's expense. Makeup. Exonerated. Teaching adults is a lever that buys happiness at essentially no growth cost. The free shelf.

The synthesis, then. Learning flips sign depending on when it is administered. Cram it into a fifteen-year-old, and you buy growth and pay in happiness. Give it to a forty-five-year-old, and you pay nothing and receive happiness.

Now, Japan's chart. Your fifteen-year-olds study among the longest hours on Earth. You have built the juku — the cram school, a rare double-schooling apparatus in which children finishing school are inserted into a second school — and at ten in the evening your station squares fill with sleepy adolescents in uniform. In social passion for children's study time, Japan is indisputably world-class.

And your forty-five-year-olds? Corporate investment in employee development (excluding on-the-job osmosis) lags the other advanced economies by a margin that your own government white papers have acknowledged for years. Acknowledged for years — savor that phrasing. Written into the white paper annually; left unchanged annually. A white paper is meant to be a trailer for coming action. This particular item is a rerun.

Lately the word "reskilling" has arrived, in katakana, accompanied by a budget line. Splendid. Though it must be said that the sensation of a problem becoming new because its name became foreign is one of this country's traditional optical illusions. What the data orders is not a name but the substance of plus 0.83: adults acquiring new capabilities inside working hours, as work. Encouraging self-funded study at night, outside working hours, is not reskilling. It is the overtime of Chapter 4 wearing a graduation cap.

So Japan's timing of educational investment is precisely inverted from what the data prescribes: concentrated at the age where learning costs happiness, absent at the age where it is free. Combine with the previous chapter and the composition acquires real flavor: Japanese adults are too busy to learn, at workplaces where the extra hours produce no growth. To think I computed one thousand and sixty-three partial correlations to arrive at this sentence. Statistics is, in the end, a technology for saying the obvious — via the scenic route, with receipts. But in public administration, the obvious and the obvious-with-receipts are different species. Only the latter gets a budget.

One more small light. "Yesterday, I learned something interesting" correlates with happiness at plus 0.80 even in the raw data. The feeling of having learned lives at nearly the same address as the feeling of being happy. The cheapest happiness a state can distribute to its citizens is a small "oh, I see." Designers of stimulus payments may wish to take note: cash, once spent, is gone; a new capability has a savings rate of one hundred percent.

Interlude II: The Price List of Happiness

Let us pause and post the shop's price list — what the checkup found on sale in the national-mood department, with tags attached. Governing is, in the end, shopping. A shopper who fills the cart without reading price tags is what we ordinarily call a government.

First, the premium shelf. Real goods, real prices. Children's study time (growth plus 0.41, at a cost of happiness minus 0.44). Tolerated inequality (growth plus 0.50, happiness minus 0.22). Purchases from this shelf are genuine, and the register will charge you. Buy deliberately.

Next, the defective-goods shelf. Extended working hours (price: happiness minus 0.57; contents of box: growth plus 0.08, i.e., packing foam). Enlarged military manpower (price: happiness minus 0.78; contents: a security debate this essay is not licensed to referee — but not mood). Big government as such (price: growth minus 0.61; contents: happiness plus 0.02, another near-empty box; see return policy regarding reverse causation, Chapter 7).

And then — the effectively free shelf. This is the shelf this essay exists to point at. Adult learning (happiness plus 0.83, no growth charge). Clean air (the lifting of a minus 0.65 penalty, no trade-off). Healthcare that reaches everyone (plus 0.52). Rule of law (plus 0.49, with a growth bonus of plus 0.34 thrown in). Higher education for the young (growth plus 0.55, no happiness charge). And labor mobility (growth plus 0.53 and happiness plus 0.37, sold as a set).

A nation's mood is stocked not in the self-help aisle but here, on the effectively free shelf. For thirty years Japan has walked past this shelf to queue, politely and punctually, at the defective-goods register. A store announcement, then: Dear customer — have you forgotten anything on your list?

Chapter 6: Air, Hospitals, and Courts — Happiness, Distributed Free of Charge

When happiness comes up, we reach for spiritual technology: gratitude journals, knowing-what-is-enough, positive thinking. The self-help shelf is a machine for outsourcing the national mood to individual willpower. The checkup's list of material conditions for a nation's mood is, by contrast, almost insultingly prosaic.

First: air. PM2.5 concentration versus happiness: minus 0.65, strengthening after deconfounding, unbudged by the removal of any single country. Under dirty air, a nation does not smile. Invert the sentence and it becomes policy: clean air is the quietest cash transfer a government can make. No deposit notification is sent, no thank-you note is received, and yet the credit posts to every citizen's account, daily.

Second: medicine. Health spending per capita looked unrelated to happiness in the raw data; after deconfounding it surfaced at plus 0.52 — another sleeper, hidden in wealth's shadow. Care that actually reaches people quietly underwrites the national mood.

Third: courts. Rule of law: happiness plus 0.49, growth plus 0.34 — an honor student of the both-ways window. Promises kept; disputes settled by procedure rather than by fist. Meritocracy — being rewarded according to one's work — runs alongside at plus 0.47.

Readers in Japan may now relax, briefly. Air, medicine, law: on all three, Japan sits in the world's upper tier. PM2.5 keeps improving; universal health insurance remains the envy of nations (the world's largest CT fleet borders on over-equipment, but as problems go, luxurious ones are preferable); the rule of law is solid. Japan, in other words, already owns happiness's foundation.

Owns the foundation — and ranks eleventh from the bottom.

Because nothing has been built on it. In the vocabulary of the preceding chapters: an immovable employment system, purchases of overtime that deliver empty boxes, and undelivered adult learning are sitting seiza, in a neat row, on top of a world-class foundation. A lot on which the groundwork was finished to perfection thirty years ago, and on which no building permit has since been filed. The real estate industry has a term for this: land banking. Japan's unhappiness is not the unhappiness of lack. It is the unhappiness of arrangement — and that one line is this checkup's entire verdict on Japan.

Chapter 7: Big Government Isn't Buying Happiness — and Inequality Is the Real Tug-of-War

So far, the findings have been pleasant. This chapter is impartially unpleasant: it will disappoint both political teams, in alphabetical order.

First, admirers of big government.

Government revenue as a share of GDP: partial correlation with growth, minus 0.61. Pension generosity (net replacement rate): minus 0.60. Total tax-and-contribution burden: minus 0.49. Each strengthens after makeup removal, survives the age-structure control, and shrugs off every leave-one-out. The larger the state's draw on the economy, the slower the growth. So far, so predictable.

Now the twist. Does big government at least buy happiness with the growth it spends? It does not. Government revenue share versus happiness: plus 0.02. Zero. Generous pensions: minus 0.13, also nothing. As far as this data can see, big government pays the growth invoice and receives no happiness merchandise. The same defective-goods contract as Chapter 4's overtime — reproduced at national scale. Citizens sign the bad deal individually; the state photocopies it. Administrative efficiency, indeed.

One honest reservation, prominently displayed: reverse causation is possible here. Slow growth may force tax ratios upward. Age structure is controlled; the arrow is not fully pinned. Friends of big government may shelter behind this reservation for now. The data will see you at the next screening.

Second, admirers of small government and unfettered competition.

The post-tax Gini coefficient — inequality after redistribution — shows growth plus 0.50 and happiness minus 0.22. Inequality is a genuine trade-off that survives everything we threw at it. Countries that tolerate inequality grow. And smile less.

Recall Chapter 1: the diligence-versus-idleness tug-of-war died on the autopsy table. The tug-of-war that survived is not about effort. It is about distribution. The dataset's grandest structure — growth versus happiness, minus 0.35 — reduces, after the makeup comes off, largely to that old, permanent question: how sharp shall we allow the differences to be? By all means, continue the ideological match. But from now on, kindly load the debate with this ammunition — plus 0.50 and minus 0.22 — rather than with "national character," "the culture of dependency," and other sentimental ordnance. Matches improve when both sides use the same caliber.

A parting gift: obedience — the degree to which a culture teaches children to obey — carries a partial correlation with growth of minus 0.50. Obedient citizens are convenient for those who govern and expensive for those who grow. Organizations whose training philosophy is "do as instructed, precisely" are invited to frame this number beside the company motto. Frames are available in the museum shop.

Chapter 8: The Country That Can't Say "I'll Manage" — Self-Efficacy, Dead Last

On the chart's final page, the numbers that hurt.

The painless one first. Japan's frequency of sexual activity ranks in the bottom tier of thirty-seven. That a national chart contains this line item at all may alarm you — statistics measures whatever holds still long enough. But the entry is less comic than it looks. Chapter 5 located happiness's substance in lived experience: learning, smiling, connection. For intimacy — one of the mood's raw materials — to run at a chronic worldwide low is a supply-chain problem. Before the demographic task force debates budgets, it might table the shortage's upstream cause, beginning with Chapter 4's working hours. Statistics does not ask much of an exhausted population; it merely writes down what exhaustion leaves.

Now the painful one.

"When I'm in a difficult situation, I can usually find a way out." Among fifteen-year-olds, Japan's agreement rate is last of thirty-seven countries: 58.6 percent. The highest is Colombia's 90.1. Denmark: 90.0.

Colombia. A country Japan outclasses on every safety statistic in the book, where nine in ten fifteen-year-olds believe they will find a way out — while in the safest, longest-lived country on Earth, four in ten believe there is none. I know of no other place in the dataset where objective danger and subjective helplessness stand so perfectly on their heads.

Self-efficacy lives, in this checkup, on the same street as the happiness factors (mathematical self-efficacy: partial correlation plus 0.47). And in this essay's view it is also the arithmetic sum of Chapters 3 through 5. In an immovable employment system, no one accumulates the experience "a bad fit can be left"; hours that purchase no growth grind the evenings away; and the adult experience of "I can now do something I couldn't" is in structurally short supply. Without a history of finding ways out, one does not believe ways out exist. Self-efficacy is not a personality trait. It is a transcript.

Time to collect the three-piece set from Chapter 2: quietly living longest, passionately cheering for idols, unable to believe one's own life can be worked out.

What is oshi-katsu — the fan's devotion? It is the full-throated support of someone else's challenge. Time, money, and emotion invested in the story of a person on a stage finding a way through. Which is to say: Japan has not lost its self-efficacy. It has transferred the entire balance into other people's accounts, where it is managed diligently and at full yield. The urgency of the fan's proverb — "support your idol while you still can" — is countersigned, on the reverse, by "I cannot support myself." The world's most fervent fan culture and the world's lowest self-efficacy sharing one chart is not, I think, a coincidence. The capacity to cheer exists, in world-class quantity. Only the direction is fixed: outward.

If so, rebuilding Japan is the modest engineering project of bending one supporter's penlight inward. Not by sermon — "believe in yourself" is a defective good; the dataset even contains a hint of it (the slogan-like "believing in myself helps me through hard times" correlates negatively with happiness after controls; belief unbacked by experience is a subprime asset). But by supply: Chapter 3's soft floor and Chapter 5's adult learning are machines for manufacturing the experience of finding a way out, at national scale. The transcript can be amended. There is one night, eventually, on which the penlight is waved at oneself.

The final irony: Japan is the world's great nation of disaster drills. Earthquake drills, fire drills, memorized evacuation routes. The only drill never conducted is the life drill — where do you run when your workplace catches fire? No one has rehearsed it. There is nowhere to rehearse to. That this missing evacuation route is precisely the both-ways lever of Chapter 3 tells you where the rebuilding must begin.

Chapter 9: The Prescription — Five Articles for Rebuilding Japan

The diagnosis has run long; the prescription will be brief. Five articles, assembled exclusively from causal candidates that survived the full interrogation. Efficacy is listed in partial correlations. Side effects are disclosed honestly. Anticipated objections come pre-answered, to save time in the examination room.

Article One. Make the chairs standable-from (labor mobility). Efficacy: growth plus 0.53, happiness plus 0.37 — the checkup's only both-ways lever. Prescription: easier separation in exchange for serious re-employment support; skills certification and pensions that travel between employers; and the retirement of the vocabulary in which changing jobs is "betrayal." Denmark calls the package flexicurity. In plainer language: a floor you can stand up onto without dying. Objection: "Mobility is a euphemism for firing people." Answer: mobility means guaranteed landings, not liberalized falls. Kicking the chair without softening the floor is not this prescription; it is assault, and contraindicated. Side effect: the personnel department loses some of its aura.

Article Two. Stop calling overtime a proof of growth (reduce hours). Efficacy: release of a minus 0.57 happiness penalty. Growth foregone: plus 0.08, i.e., nothing. Prescription: evaluate output, not occupancy; permit business correspondence that does not open by apologizing for the recipient's presumed busyness. Korea is running the fifty-two-hour experiment at its own expense; free-ride on the results. Objection: "The floor staff can't cope as it is." Answer: floors that can't cope are usually floors where people are misplaced, not missing — see Article One, with which this article is co-prescribed. Side effect: "busy" ceases to function as a greeting, and Japanese conversation loses one of its load-bearing pleasantries.

Article Three. Distribute learning to adults (employer training and re-learning). Efficacy: happiness plus 0.83, the strongest substantive mood factor in the data. No growth charge. Prescription: strong tax preference for corporate training investment; personal learning accounts with no strings; training delivered inside working hours, as work (outside hours it violates Article Two). Redirect, say, three-tenths of the national passion for fifteen-year-olds' cram schools toward forty-five-year-olds. Print "share of citizens who learned something yesterday" beside GDP in the statistical yearbook. Objection: "We'll train them and they'll leave." Answer: Article One converts that question's grammar. In a society where every firm trains, the loser is not the firm whose trained workers leave but the firm no trained worker joins. A company that doesn't train retains, in the long run, only the untrained. Side effect: work stops during training hours. A portion of that work was scheduled for cancellation under Article Two anyway.

Article Four. Keep polishing the foundation (air, medicine, law). Efficacy: minus 0.65 avoided; plus 0.52; plus 0.49. Prescription: Japan already leads here; maintain and fine-tune. Before boasting about the world's largest CT fleet, route its idle hours into prevention. Objection: "Why list what's already done?" Answer: to confiscate the excuse. With a world-class foundation on record, the absence of a building can no longer be blamed on the ground. Side effect: none — though self-congratulation on finished items is limited to once daily, before meals.

Article Five. Continue the inequality argument — with new ammunition. Efficacy: this is less a prescription than a rulebook for the correct fight. Growth plus 0.50, happiness minus 0.22: inequality is a real trade-off, and statistics does not adjudicate real trade-offs. How sharp to let differences grow, and where to sand them down, is a question democracy must sweat over honestly. Prescription: both teams must, however, leave one document on the table throughout — the audit showing that big government has not been buying happiness (plus 0.02). Compete on the direction of spending (Article Three, for instance), not its tonnage. Objection: "Don't reduce politics to numbers." Answer: politics without numbers is reduced to volume. Ask the citizens with quiet voices which reduction they prefer. Side effect: speeches shorten on both sides. The electorate will cope.

The five articles share one spirit. Japan must move from a country that accumulates to a country that circulates — people, money, learning, seats. What this country lacks is not resources but rotation. The world's longest life expectancy is, translated, the world's longest time on the game clock. A player with the longest clock in the tournament, declining to move any pieces, waiting for time to expire: in shogi this is a borderline violation called stalling. Fortunately, nations play without a clock limit. The move can still be made.

Chapter 10: Special Feature — The Objections This Essay Will Receive, and How to Enjoy Them Properly

Every rebuilding plan attracts returns and refunds. We therefore pre-sort the major complaint categories and attach responses. Readers are invited to identify their own complaint number before proceeding to the register.

Complaint One: "Thirty-seven countries? That's nothing."

Entirely correct, and confessed throughout. One note, however: thirty-seven is not a small sample of advanced economies. It is nearly the entire population of them. The sample is not undersized; the planet is underdeveloped. Address complaints regarding the planet's progress to the relevant window.

Complaint Two: "Happiness differs by culture; international comparison is meaningless."

Half right — which is why this essay amputated the Latin cheer and the Nordic contentment with dummy variables and reported only what remained. To reject all international comparison on cultural grounds is to refuse a medical checkup on the grounds of having a unique constitution. Constitutions are real. Blood pressure is still worth measuring.

Complaint Three: "You yourself keep saying correlation isn't causation."

I do, and will again. But that famous sentence has a correct usage and an incorrect one. Correct: "…therefore, let us climb toward causation by procedure." Incorrect: "…therefore nothing can be known," followed immediately by an anecdote about one's brother-in-law. Correlation is not causation; anecdotes at the izakaya are not even correlation. This essay took the former road as far as observational data permits. Beyond this point stand natural experiments and panel data; the itinerary is filed in the epilogue.

Complaint Four: "Labor mobility is a neoliberal plot" — and, from the opposite register, "adult-learning subsidies are big-government bloat."

Note with pleasure that these two returns arrive simultaneously, from opposite checkout lanes, for the same product. The prescription looks market-flavored on employment and state-flavored on learning and foundations. Good. Data swears loyalty to no team. Plus 0.53 is written in neither the vocabulary of the right nor the left; it is a number. Readers who perceive ideology in a number are encouraged to have not their eyes but their ideology examined.

Complaint Five: "Japan has its own way of doing things."

The most honored complaint in the store — a bestseller for thirty years. One clarification, though. If "Japan's own way" consists concretely of the world's highest lifetime-employment rate, working hours that purchase no growth, and learning that is never delivered to adults — then it is not a way of doing things. It is the symptom chart, recited with pride. Naming one's symptoms "heritage" is any patient's right, but it has never once been recorded as a treatment.

Complaint Six: "Too many jokes. Write seriously."

This one deserves a serious answer. The literature on Japan's stagnation has been serious for thirty years — serious white papers, serious proposals, serious expert panels — and the harvest of all that seriousness is an annual rerun. The prose style, at least, can practice what Article One preaches and change jobs. Besides: laughter is the first honest admission that one's legs, after all this seiza, have gone numb.

Epilogue: The Aesthetics of the Fine Print

Finally, the product label, printed without abbreviation.

First: this is correlation, not proven causation. No method in this universe extracts proof of causation from thirty-seven observational data points. What this essay delivers is the elite of causal candidates — survivors of five layers of deconfounding, thirty-seven interrogations, dose-response and sibling-consistency checks, and a verification of temporal order. Elite candidates. Not a verdict.

Second: the sample is thirty-seven countries. Hurl a thousand indicators at it, and phantom correlations will arise by arithmetic necessity. This checkup produced at least one specimen so absurd — HIV incidence "improving" both growth and happiness — that we mounted it as the museum's centerpiece, deliberately unretouched. Humility before data is not a posture; it is an exhibit.

Third: the falsification calendar is attached. The outcome of Korea's working-hours cap. Replications of Danish-style flexicurity elsewhere. A domestic panel: do Japanese prefectures with higher job mobility show higher mood and higher growth? These hypotheses are published with their right to fail fully guaranteed. Should they fail, this essay will be demoted to "an enjoyable read," and will accept the demotion gracefully. Distrust any rebuilding plan without fine print. History's fine-print-free rebuilding plans have reliably rebuilt exactly one thing: their authors' self-regard.

And yet — I find hope in these results, for one simple reason: Japan's problem is a problem of arrangement. No national character needs replacing; no resource needs discovering; no miracle technology needs inventing. Lay a floor that permits standing; look away from the clock; hand adults something new to learn. All of it can be nudged by ordinary institutions, starting tomorrow. Rebuilding plans that demand the remodeling of souls should be returned unopened. Rebuilding plans that demand the rearrangement of furniture can be executed by any competent contractor — and Japan's contractors are excellent.

One last small light from the data, and I will put down the pen. "Yesterday, I learned something interesting" — plus 0.80 with happiness — has almost no correlation with national wealth. The poor country's citizen and the rich country's citizen are equally eligible for yesterday's small "oh, I see." The strongest correlate of happiness is being handed out daily, free, outside the jurisdiction of GDP. For a country carrying thirty years of stagnation, no fact could be more convenient. The first day of reconstruction requires no act of parliament; it can begin this evening, with one new thing learned before going home. As a national rebuilding program this may sound undersized — until one remembers that this is the country that compounded small improvements for forty years into the world's longest life. The same patience, redirected from the length of life to the point of living, will do.

In the world's finest hospital bed, holding the world's longest game clock, Japan is still lying down. The bedside monitor reads normal; the intravenous drip falls punctually; in the corridor, the world's largest population of pharmacists passes quietly to and fro. The attending physician appends one line to the chart and ends the consultation.

— Findings: ambulation unimpaired. Awaiting only the patient's decision to walk.

(End)

Data: World Effect Benchmark (37 OECD countries × 1,193 factors; GDP growth = 1999–2019 average; happiness = positive affect 2020–2023; PISA 2018; PIAAC and others). Partial correlations control for log GDP per capita, post-communist, Latin, Nordic, and working-age population share. Correlation is not causation — this essay spends fourteen chapters apologizing for that, and then says only what can be said anyway.
